Members

Members of the center are an interdisciplinary team that assess the factors that affect how early experiences set the stage for later pathology and the interventions that can be adopted to prevent this cycle of adverse outcomes. Using a variety of approaches to explore these issues, our members conduct research at multiple levels of analysis to identify the mechanisms responsible for the development of stress-related pathology as well as how to buffer these effects and/or increase resilience. Efforts are ongoing to develop prevention and treatment strategies relevant to stress-related psychological disorders that range from depression, anxiety disorders, PTSD, and suicide, as well as the constellation of physical illnesses, such as obesity, type 2 diabetes, and cardiovascular, infectious and neurodegenerative diseases. Considerable attention is also devoted to the repercussions related to chronic illness and chronic pain.

Dr. Kim Hellemans
About

Dr Kim Hellemans is interested in exploring how stress and trauma predict later life mental health vulnerability, with a focus on substance use and addictions. Here current research focus is on understanding factors that relate to student mental health, and in particular, how patterns and motivations of cannabis use relate to both mental health symptoms and academic outcomes among university students. Dr Hellemans also has significant expertise in knowledge translation and knowledge mobilization, and has developed a suite of animations depicting diverse aspects of brain function for the lay public as well as for student use. She is the co-host of the award-winning podcast "Minding the Brain", along with her colleague Dr Jim Davies.


Current Projects

Assessment of stigmatization and mental health associated with nonalcohol drinking undergraduate students in Canadian universities Cannabis Use, Mental Health, and Social Media among undergraduates amid the COVID-19 crisis Mental Health, Substance Use and Coping during the COVID-19 pandemic among Canadian Graduate Students

Dr. Robyn McQuaid
About

Dr. McQuaid’s research takes a multidisciplinary approach that examines the impacts of stress and trauma on mental health. Specifically, she explores how adverse experiences and biomarkers, such as genetics, epigenetics and inflammation, come together to promote depression and suicide. A key feature of her research is to take a personalized approach to understanding mental health disorders by considering gender, culture and environmental experiences.

Dr. Hymie Anismans
About

The research conducted by Professor Anisman has focused on the impact of stressful events on the neurobiological, immune, and microbial processes and their ultimate effects on vulnerability and resilience to diverse diseases. A primary focus of his research has been appreciation of the psychosocial processes that moderate the effects of chronic stressor experiences on illness and wellness.

Dr. Paul Villeneuve
About

Dr. Villeneuve is currently leading research that is investigating the impacts of urban built environments, including air pollution, noise and greenness, on chronic disease and depression. He works with large scale administrative datasets that are housed with Statistic’s Canada Research Data centers. His research has funded by the Canadian Institutes of Health Research, the Canadian Nuclear Safety Commission, and the Alzheimer’s Society of Canada.

Sarah Everts
About

Sarah Everts is the CTV Chair in Digital Science Journalism and an Associate Professor in the School of Journalism and Communication. She joined Carleton University in 2019 after more than a decade in Berlin, Germany, where she reported on science and technology for a variety of publications including Scientific American, New Scientist, Smithsonian, Chemical & Engineering News, and others. Her work has garnered several awards and accolades, such as inclusion in the 2017 Best American Science and Nature Writing anthology. She is currently leading a quantitative project to assess the Canadian news media's use of stigmatizing words to describe people who use drugs, from the beginning of the opioid crisis through to the present. Her hope is to inspire the news media towards stigma-free coverage of people who use drugs, to develop anti-stigma workshops for journalists, and to help news media craft harm-reduction-inspired style guides. She has a Master of Journalism from Carleton, a Master of Science in Chemistry from the University of British Columbia, and a Bachelor of Science in Biophysics from the University of Guelph.
